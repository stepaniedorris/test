BEAZLEY’S FIDUCIARY OBLIGATIONS

As you know:

There is an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in every contract [including insurance policies] that neither party will do anything which will injure the right of the other to receive the benefits of the agreement.’ Therefore, ‘an insurer . . . who refuses to accept a reasonable settlement within the policy limits in violation of its duty to consider in good faith the interest of the insured in the settlement, is liable for the entire judgment against the insured even if it exceeds the policy limits.’


Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 573 (citations omitted, emphasis added, brackets and ellipsis by the Court). In other words:

‘[A]n insurance policy’s express promise to defend and indemnify the insured against injury claims implies a duty to settle third party claims in an appropriate case.’ ‘More specifically, the insurer must settle within policy limits when there is substantial likelihood of recovery in excess of those limits. The duty to settle is implied in law to protect the insured from exposure to liability in excess of coverage as a result of the insurer's gamble — on which only the insured might lose.’


Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co., (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390, 401(citations omitted, brackets by the Court). In short, “gambling with an insured’s money is a breach of fiduciary duty.”

R.C. Wegman Constr. Co. v. Admiral Ins. Co., 629 F.3d 724, 729 (7th Cir. 2011) (compiling cases). See gen. Allan D. Windt, Insurance Claims & Disputes: Representation of Insurance Companies & Insureds § 5:1 (6th ed). Such a breach will expose Beazley not only to a judgment in excess of the policy’s indemnity limits, Hedayati v. Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club (2021) 67 Cal. App. 5th 833, 845, but also to full tort damages and, in appropriate cases, punitive damages as well. Major v. Western Home Ins. Co. (2009) 169 Cal. App. 4th 1197, 1224.

  1. The Oregon Rule

    As we noted, Don Vonk is charging Releasees with both negligence and gross negligence in connection with the subject allision.

    As in any negligence case, the plaintiff in a maritime allision suit bears the burden of proof by a preponderance of the evidence. Zerega Ave. Realty Corp. v. Hornbeck Offshore Transp., LLC, 571 F.3d 206, 212 (2d Cir. 2009). The plaintiff must prove essentially the same elements as a land-based negligence claim at common law: that the defendant breached a legal duty, causing the injury sustained by the plaintiff. See In re Cooper/T. Smith, 929 F.2d 1073, 1077 (5th Cir. 1991) (per curiam); see also Evergreen Int'l, S.A. v. Norfolk Dredging Co., 531 F.3d 302, 308 (4th Cir. 2008). The duty of care owed by a moving vessel to a stationary object such as a bridge [or a seawall] is reasonable care under the circumstances.


    Fischer v. S/Y NERAIDA, 508 F.3d 586, 593 (11th Cir. 2007). Experience and common sense counsel that a moving vessel does not ordinarily strike a stationary object unless the vessel is mishandled in some way. [In re] Am. Milling, 409 F.3d [1005] 1018 [8th Cir. 2005]. As such, the plaintiff in an allision case may invoke the Oregon rule, which creates a rebuttable presumption that a moving vessel breached its duty of care when it allides with a stationary object. Id. at 1012; Union Pac. R.R. Co. v. Kirby Inland Marine, Inc. of Miss., 296 F.3d 671, 673 (8th Cir. 2002); see The Oregon, 158 U.S. 186, 197 (1895). The Oregon presumption satisfies the plaintiff's prima facie case, shifting the burden of proof on issues of duty and breach to the defendant. City of Chicago v. M/V Morgan, 375 F.3d 563, 572-73 (7th Cir. 2004).


    To rebut the Oregon presumption, the moving vessel may prove one of three things: that ‘(1) the moving vessel used all reasonable care to avoid the allision and was therefore without fault, (2) the stationary object was at fault, or (3) the allision occurred because of an “inevitable accident.”’ Am. Milling, 409 F.3d at 1018 (quoting Bunge Corp. v. M/V Furness Bridge, 558 F.2d 790, 795 (5th Cir. 1977)).


    Dakota, Minn. & Eastern R.R. Corp. v. Ingram Barge Co., 918 F.3d 967, 971-972 (8th Cir. 2019).\ (emphasis added) (original brackets omitted).

    The Oregon Rule -- and its close cousins res ipsa loquitur1 and the Pennsylvania Rule2 -- leave Releasors with the impossible task of proving that Naiad and her master, Capt. Bryan Everitt, used all reasonable care when they violated the Inland Rules of Navigation, careened into Pier 39’s West Basin at a speed of more than seven knots, allided with a stationary sea wall, tore Naiad’s starboard-main-deck hand rail free from her hull, launched that hand rail backwards into Don Vonk, and sent Mr. Vonk cartwheeling overboard into the Bay.


    1 The doctrine of ipsa loquitur properly applies in admiralty if: “1) the injured party was without fault; 2) the instrumentality causing the injury was under the exclusive control of the defendant; and 3) the mishap is of a type that ordinarily does not occur in the absence of negligence.” United States v. Nassau Marine Corp., 778 F.2d 1111, 1115-16 (5th Cir. 1985) (citations and internal ellipses omitted).

    2 Under the Pennsylvania Rule, “when, as in this case, a ship at the time of a collision is in actual violation of a statutory rule intended to prevent collisions, it is no more than a reasonable presumption that the fault, if not the sole cause, was at least a contributory cause of the disaster. In such a case the burden rests upon the ship of showing not merely that her fault might not have been one of the causes, or that it probably was not, but that it could not have been.” Steamship Pennsylvania v. Troop, 86 U.S. 125, 136 (1873); see also MacDonald v. Kahikolu, Ltd., 581 F.3d 970, 971 (9th Cir., 2009); Ishizaki Kisen Co. v. United States, 510 F.2d 875, 879 (9th Cir. 1975). What is more, the ship must bear that burden with “clear and convincing evidence.” MacDonald, 581 F.3d at 971.


    The type of trial witness plaintiffs’ lawyers fear most in res ipsa cases like this one is the diffident wrongdoer who admits his or her mistake with a straightforward answer and an apologetic smile. Such a witness keeps the damage verdict down by evincing contrition and forestalling juror indignation. Capt. Everitt is not such a witness.

The Releasees’ Defenses

Releasees hope to wall themselves off from a likely excess judgment in this case with Ehart

v. Lahaina Divers, Inc., 92 F.4th 844 (9th Cir. 2024) and the pre-accident liability waiver they require all their passengers to sign. But that hope is forlorn.

M/V Naiad is a so-called “T boat.” She is an “inspected passenger vessel” of less than one- hundred tons and is therefore subject to regulation under Subchapter T of the Coast Guard regulations. 46 C.F.R. Parts 175-185. In Ehart, the Ninth Circuit held that the statutory bar in § 27 of the Limitation of Liability Act (“LOLA”) prohibiting “‘a vessel transporting passengers between ports in the United States” from foisting a contractual provision on those passengers “limiting the liability of the owner, master, or agent for personal injury or death caused by the negligence or fault of the owner or the owner's employees or agents,’” does not apply to vessels “transporting passengers away from and back to a single port.” 92 F.4th at 851 (quoting 46 U.S.C. 90527(a)(1)). Because the Naiad typically transports mourners away from and back to Pier 39, Releasors have persuaded themselves that the liability release they obtained from on Naiad’s passengers was valid. But Releasors are wrong for three reasons.

First, on December 23, 2022, almost a full year before the subject allision, Congress amended 46 U.S.C. § 30502(b) in the wake of the Conception tragedy, see P.L. 117-263, Div K, Title CXV, Subtitle A, § 11503(c), 136 Stat. 4130, and removed T-boats like Conception and Naiad from the reach of LOLA and § 90527(a)(1). As a consequence, the Ninth Circuit’s holding in Ehart has no application here. Neither does § 90527(a)(1). Once Congress pulled T-boats like Naiad out.of LOLA and § 90527, those vessels fell back under the traditional, common-law rule. It has been settled for more than a century under that rule that “common carriers” may not impose exculpatory agreements on their passengers. See e.g. Liverpool & Great Western Steam Co. v. Phenix Ins. Co., 129 U.S. 397, 439-440 (1889) (“Liverpool Steam”) (“By the common law of England and America before the Declaration of Independence, recognized by the weight of English authority for a half century afterwards, and upheld by decisions of the highest courts of many States of the Union, common carriers could not stipulate for immunity for their own or their servants’ negligence.”); The Kensington, 183 U.S. 263, 268 (1902) (“It is settled in the courts of the United States that exemptions limiting carriers from responsibility for the negligence of themselves or their servants are both unjust and unreasonable, and will be deemed wanting in the


element of voluntary assent; and, besides, that such conditions are in conflict with public policy. This doctrine was announced so long ago, and has been so frequently reiterated, that it is elementary.”); The Oregon, 133 F. 609, 630 (9th Cir. 1904) (“With respect to the other provision of the contract, relieving the carrier from responsibility of the carrier provided he has used due diligence to make the vessel seaworthy, it is perhaps sufficient to say that this provision, if it means anything, is also inapplicable to this case. But if, by any construction of its terms, it can be held applicable, it is clearly void by reason of being against public policy.”) (citing Liverpool Steam, supra). Those lodestar precedents remain as strong as ever today. See e.g. Great Lakes Ins. SE v. Raiders Retreat Realty Co., LLC, 601 U.S. 65, 76 (2024) (“For example, The Kensington declined to enforce a choice-of-law clause because the chosen law would have released a carrier from liability for negligence—a result that maritime law forbids.”) (emphasis added) (discussing “established” maritime rules).

Naiad is clearly a “common carrier” within the meaning of that rule. As the Supreme Court explained with respect to a vessel that towed logs on Puget Sound, “[any]one who undertakes for hire to transport from place to place the property of others who may choose to employ him is a common carrier.” Washington ex rel. Stimson Lumber Co. v. Kuykendall, 275 U.S. 207, 211 (1927). More particularly: “Carriers by water, holding themselves out to the public as carriers of passengers, are common carriers of passengers.” Semon v. Royal Indem. Co., 279 F.2d 737, 739 (5th Cir. 1960). “‘The public,’ however, “‘does not mean everybody all the time.’” Med-Trans Corp. v. Benton, 581 F.Supp.2d 721, 733 (E.D.N.C. 2008) (quoting Terminal Taxicab Co. v. Kutz, 241 U.S. 252, 255 (1916). As the Eighth Circuit explained when it held that “an ‘air ambulance’ service that provides emergency medical transportation by helicopter” was a common carrier, Riegelsberger v. Air Evac EMS, Inc., 970 F.3d 1061, 1063 (8th Cir. 2020), “a carrier is still ‘common’ even if it carves out a specialized niche in the marketplace that serves only ‘a definable segment of the public.’” Id. at 1064 (citations omitted). That definition fits Naiad like a glove. That vessel and her owners offer offshore scatterings and “peace of mind” to anyone who has lost a loved one; their advertising trumpets that they have been “TRUSTED BY OVER 1 MILLION FAMILIES SINCE 1973.” See http://neptunesociety.com. Neptune touts the “affordability, flexibility, and simplicity” of cremation, id., and, like the common carrier in Med-Trans Corp., supra, “which presente[d] itself as ‘one of the nation’s leading providers of air ambulance


services,’” 581 F. Supp. 2d at 726, Neptune “makes its services available to any ‘who may choose to employ [it].’” Id. at 733 (quoting Kuykendall, 275 U.S. at 211; see also Fleming v. Chicago Cartage Co., 160 F.2d 992, 998 (7th Cir. 1947)

Second, even if M/V Naiad had not been a common carrier, the exculpatory agreement Releasors attempted to impose on that vessel’s passengers calls on each passenger to:

[A]gree to release, indemnify and hold harmless S.E. Combined Services of California, Inc, the vessel, its affiliates and owners, and their respective officers, directors, shareholders, employees, representatives, successors, subsidiaries, parents, agents and assigns from any and all liabilities, claims or causes of action arising out of, or in any way connected with my participation on the boat and from any and all responsibility or liability for any and all injuries or damages. I further agree on behalf of myself, my heirs and assigns to indemnify and hold harmless the released parties for any and all causes of action arising as a consequence of any incidents which might occur as a consequence of my participation in any activities with or involving the released parties.

Deposition Exhibit 8 at S.E. Combined 0028 (emphasis added). That sweeping language fails to distinguish between ordinary negligence and gross negligence. Unlike ordinary negligence, which consists of a simple failure to use “ordinary care,” Kermarec v. Compagnie Generale, 358 U.S. 625, 630, gross negligence comprises either “the want of even scant care” or “the intentional failure to perform a manifest duty in reckless disregard of the consequences as affecting the life or property of another.” Royal Ins. Co. of Am. v. Southwest Marine, 194 F.3d 1009, 1015 (9th Cir. 1999). It is apodictic “that a party to a maritime contract should not be permitted to shield itself contractually from liability for gross negligence.” Id. at 1016.

Which brings us to the third flaw in the subject release.

In maritime contract disputes, federal courts apply general principles of contract law. However, to the extent that it is not inconsistent with admiralty principles, state contract law may be applicable to maritime contracts.

Drinnon Marine, LLC v. Four Rivers Towing of Ala., LLC, 549 F. Supp. 3d 505, 516 (E. D. La. 2021) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). As the District of Hawaii explained in Courtney v. Pacific Adventures, Inc., 5 F.Supp.2d 874 (D.Haw. 1998), when it applied Hawaii state law to strike down the release that a dive instructor named Stafford tried to impose on the passengers of the dive boat Kai Nula:

‘[T]the public interest is at stake when a party attempts to contract to exempt himself for harm caused by his gross negligence.’ Wheelock v. Sport Kites, Inc.,


839 F. Supp. 730, 736 (D. Haw. 1993). Thus, an agreement is void that limits liability for gross negligence.


The Release in this case limits from liability injuries ‘caused by negligence or gross negligence or otherwise.’ The Release is clearly illegal with respect to gross negligence. Whether the Release is invalid in its entirety depends on whether it is severable. See Ai v. Frank Huff Agency, Ltd., 61 Haw. 607, 619, 607 P.2d 1304 (1980) (‘It is well settled under ordinary contract law, however, that a partially illegal contract may be upheld if the illegal portion is severable from the part which is legal.’) (citation omitted).


In Farina v. Mt. Bachelor, Inc., the Ninth Circuit addressed the severability of a similar release contract. 66 F.3d 233, 235 (9th Cir. 1995). In that case, the release stated, ‘This release and indemnity agreement shall apply to claims based upon negligence and for any other theory of recovery.’ Id. at 234. The court found that under Oregon law, a release for liability for more than ordinary negligence violates public policy. Id. at 235. The court concluded that the entire release was invalid because the language of the release did not manifest an intention by the parties to sever the contract. Id. at 236. The court observed, ‘In one simple, broad sentence, the defendant sought to exculpate itself for any and all claims. It is not our role to enforce only part of the release clause where it is not obvious from the language of the clause that the parties intended the clause to be severable.’ Id.; but see Wheelcock, 839 F. Supp. at 736 (concluding without addressing the severability issue and in a case of first impression that a release is void only to the extent that it attempts to relieve the defendants of liability for their gross negligence).


Like the defendant in Farina, Stafford has attempted in one sentence to exempt himself from all claims against him. There is no manifestation of an intent to sever some claims from others. Thus, the Release is not severable and is therefore invalid in its entirety.


Id. at 882 (brackets, evidentiary citations, and ellipses omitted).


The same is true in our case under California law. See e.g. Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011)191 Cal. App. 4th 826, 829 (“an agreement that would remove a party’s obligation to adhere to even a minimal standard of care, thereby sheltering aggravated misconduct, is unenforceable as against public”); City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal. 4th 747, 787 (“all releases of future liability for gross negligence, whether express or implied, are generally unenforceable in California as contrary to the public policy of this state, and [] the specific release of future negligence liability utilized by the city in this case, to the extent it implicitly encompasses gross negligence, “violates public policy and is unenforceable”). Since


there is nothing in Naiad’s release to suggest that its indiscriminate waiver of future liability for gross negligence was meant to be severable, the entire waiver is void. See e.g. Morey v.

Paladini (1922) 187 Cal. 727, 738.


Since all parties agree that Don Vonk did nothing wrong, Releasees have no viable defenses herein.

Under the well-settled maritime rule:


[A] bystander who is in a zone of physical danger and who, because of the defendant’s negligence, has reasonable fear for his own safety is given a right of action for physical injury or illness resulting from emotional distress. This rule does not require that the bystander suffer a physical impact or injury at the time of the negligent act, but it does require that he must have been in such proximity to the accident in which the direct victim was physically injured that there was a high risk to him of physical impact.

Stacy, 609 F.3d at 1043 (internal quotation marks and citation omitted). All twenty-six of the other Releasors shared the zone of physical danger with Don Vonk aboard the Naiad when Capt.
